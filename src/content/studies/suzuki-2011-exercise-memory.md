Suzuki and Bhattacharya review the behavioral and neurobiological evidence for exercise-induced improvements in cognition, focusing on the acute (single-session) and long-term effects of aerobic exercise on memory, attention, and mood. The acute effects — measurable within 1–2 hours of a 20-minute moderate-intensity exercise bout — include improvements in working memory, episodic memory, and sustained attention, mediated by increased release of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), noradrenaline, serotonin, and dopamine.

The long-term effects reviewed include increased hippocampal volume in regular exercisers — a finding that is remarkable because hippocampal volume typically shrinks with aging, and exercise appears to reverse this trajectory. Suzuki's own experimental work shows that these effects are dose-responsive and observable even in sedentary adults who begin a new exercise program. The practical implication is that aerobic exercise functions as an acute cognitive enhancer as well as a long-term neuroprotective practice, making the timing of exercise relative to demanding cognitive work an important design variable.
