Grant's 2013 synthesis draws on his research at Wharton and collaborating institutions to build the empirical case that giving behavior — contributing to others without keeping score — is a strong predictor of long-term professional success. His most striking finding is that givers are disproportionately represented at both the bottom and top of productivity distributions: the least successful people are often givers who give indiscriminately and get exploited, while the most successful people are often givers who give strategically — high-impact, low-cost contributions that build network capital and meaning without burning out.

The research documents specific mechanisms through which giving produces advantages: information access (givers build broader networks and receive more referrals), reputation effects (givers are sought for collaboration and leadership), and meaning (helping others directly activates the motivational circuitry associated with purpose). Grant's own lab experiments, as well as field studies in call centers showing that brief exposure to beneficiaries increased fundraising performance by 171%, demonstrate that giving behavior has causal effects on performance outcomes. The Five-Minute Give experiment is designed to preserve the benefits of giving behavior while avoiding the exploitation trap through the strict time boundary.
