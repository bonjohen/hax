Matthew Walker's 2017 synthesis of the sleep science literature represents the most comprehensive lay-accessible review of what is known about sleep's role in health and cognition. Drawing on his own research and the broader field, Walker documents the effects of sleep on memory consolidation (particularly the role of slow-wave and REM sleep in different types of learning), immune function, cardiovascular health, emotional regulation, and metabolic processes. The book makes a compelling case that the minimum effective dose for most adults is 7–9 hours, and that the productivity logic of sleeping less is precisely backwards: sleep-deprived cognition is severely impaired in ways that the sleep-deprived person cannot accurately self-assess.

Walker also describes the mechanisms by which consistent sleep timing and pre-sleep behavior (light exposure, temperature, screen use) shapes sleep architecture. These findings form the scientific foundation for the sleep wind-down experiment. While the book is a popular synthesis rather than a primary study, Walker's underlying research — including papers on sleep and emotion (Walker & van der Helm, 2009) and sleep and memory (Walker et al., 2003) — is peer-reviewed and widely cited.
