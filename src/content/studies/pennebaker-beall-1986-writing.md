Pennebaker and Beall's 1986 study published in the Journal of Abnormal Psychology is the founding paper of the expressive writing research tradition. In a controlled experiment, undergraduate students were randomly assigned to write for 15–20 minutes per day over four days about either their deepest thoughts and feelings regarding a traumatic experience, facts about a traumatic experience without emotional engagement, or a trivial topic. Students who wrote about emotions and facts combined (the full expressive writing condition) visited the health center significantly less frequently in the six months following the study and reported better health than controls.

Subsequent studies by Pennebaker and dozens of other researchers have extended these findings to immune function (increased T-lymphocyte proliferation), depression and anxiety reduction, academic performance in students, re-employment rates in laid-off executives, and pain management in arthritis patients. The robustness of the effect across populations and outcome types is unusual in psychological research. Pennebaker's theoretical account — that unprocessed emotional experiences create a chronic inhibitory burden that writing helps discharge — has been partially supported by neuroimaging research showing reduced amygdala activation to trauma-related stimuli after expressive writing.
