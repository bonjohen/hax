Dweck's 2006 book synthesizes her decades of experimental research on "implicit theories" of intelligence — the beliefs people hold about whether abilities are fixed or malleable. Her foundational experiments showed that praising children for effort versus intelligence produced divergent patterns: effort-praised children sought more challenging tasks, persisted longer after failure, and achieved more over time, while intelligence-praised children avoided challenge and interpreted failure as evidence of permanent inadequacy.

The mindset framework has been tested in academic settings (students given growth mindset training showing significant GPA improvements), athletic contexts, workplace settings, and longitudinal studies tracking outcomes over years. Dweck's lab has also investigated the neural correlates of mindset: growth-minded individuals show different EEG patterns in response to errors, exhibiting greater N2 (error detection) amplitude and faster return to task engagement after mistakes. The practical intervention — teaching people that the brain is changeable and that effort builds capacity — has been replicated across multiple randomized trials. Critics have noted replication challenges in some specific effect-size claims, but the broad pattern that mindset beliefs shape learning behavior is among the most robust findings in educational and motivational psychology.
