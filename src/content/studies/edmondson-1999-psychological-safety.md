Edmondson's 1999 study in Administrative Science Quarterly is the foundational empirical paper on psychological safety in organizational teams. Working with 51 work teams in a manufacturing company, Edmondson developed the now widely-used seven-item psychological safety scale and demonstrated that team psychological safety — the shared belief that interpersonal risk-taking is safe — predicted team learning behavior, which in turn predicted team performance. Crucially, the study showed that the relationship between psychological safety and performance was mediated by learning behavior: safe teams learned faster, and teams that learned faster performed better.

Edmondson's subsequent research extended these findings to hospital teams (where psychological safety predicted medication error reporting), technology teams, and multinational organizations. The consistent finding across contexts is that psychological safety is not a soft cultural amenity — it is a structural condition that determines whether the information a team needs to adapt and improve actually surfaces in practice. Her research forms the empirical foundation for Google's Project Aristotle, which independently identified psychological safety as the top predictor of team effectiveness across 180 Google teams.
