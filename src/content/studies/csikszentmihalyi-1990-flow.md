Csikszentmihalyi's foundational work on flow draws on the experience sampling method (ESM), in which participants carry pagers (later phones) and are prompted at random intervals to report their current activity, attention state, and emotional experience. This method, applied across thousands of participants in multiple countries over 25 years, allowed Csikszentmihalyi to map the conditions under which people report optimal experience — states of total absorption, effortless action, and intrinsic reward.

His analysis identified the challenge-skill balance as the key structural condition: flow occurs in the corridor between boredom (challenge too low) and anxiety (challenge too high). Additional conditions include clear goals and immediate feedback, both of which reduce ambiguity and allow attention to fully focus on the activity. The flow state is associated with high subjective engagement, a distorted sense of time, loss of self-consciousness, and what Csikszentmihalyi calls autotelic experience — activity that is intrinsically rewarding regardless of external outcomes. Subsequent neuroscience research has associated flow with reduced activity in the default mode network (reduced self-referential processing) and transient hypofrontality (reduced prefrontal monitoring), providing a neurobiological account consistent with the phenomenological reports.
