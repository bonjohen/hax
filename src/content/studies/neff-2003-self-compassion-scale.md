Neff's 2003 paper in Self and Identity introduced the self-compassion construct and the Self-Compassion Scale — now one of the most widely used measures in positive psychology research. Neff defines self-compassion as comprising three interacting components: self-kindness (treating oneself warmly rather than harshly during failure), common humanity (recognizing that suffering is a shared human experience rather than a sign of personal inadequacy), and mindfulness (holding painful thoughts in balanced awareness rather than over-identifying with them or suppressing them).

In the initial validation studies, self-compassion predicted lower depression, anxiety, and self-criticism, and higher emotional intelligence, happiness, and optimism — while being largely independent of self-esteem. Subsequent research by Neff and others has shown that self-compassion is a more stable predictor of wellbeing than self-esteem (which fluctuates with performance) and that, contrary to common intuition, high self-compassion does not reduce motivation, accountability, or standards. People high in self-compassion are more likely to acknowledge mistakes honestly and take corrective action, because they do not need to protect their ego from the implications of failure.
