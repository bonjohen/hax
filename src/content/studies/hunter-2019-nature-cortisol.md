Hunter, Gillespie, and Chen's 2019 study published in Frontiers in Psychology is notable for measuring cortisol in the context of participants' actual daily lives rather than under laboratory conditions. Using ecological momentary assessment, 36 urban residents provided salivary cortisol samples before and after nature doses ranging from 0 to 60 minutes. The results showed a significant cortisol reduction after nature exposure, with the steepest decline occurring between 20 and 30 minutes — suggesting a dose-response relationship with a practical floor around 20 minutes.

Importantly, the study controlled for time of day (which affects cortisol independently), physical activity level, and social context, isolating the nature exposure itself as the predictor. The cortisol reduction was observable across a range of urban natural settings — parks, waterfront areas, and tree-lined streets — suggesting the effect is robust to variation in the nature setting. These findings provide a biologically grounded argument for the minimum effective dose in nature exposure interventions and support the design of the Nature Micro-Dose experiment around 20-minute sessions.
