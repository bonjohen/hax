The Three-Item Gratitude Journal comes from Shawn Achor's synthesis of positive psychology research. The core finding is that training your brain to scan for positives — rather than negatives — can create lasting shifts in baseline happiness and work performance.

## How It Works

By writing down three new things each day, you force your brain to actively search for positive experiences. Over 21 days, this scanning pattern becomes more automatic, shifting your default perspective from problem-focused to opportunity-focused.

## Why Three Items and Why New

Three is enough to require genuine reflection without being burdensome. The "new each day" rule prevents autopilot — you can't just list "family, health, job" every day. This forces genuine attention to the day's specific experiences.

## What the Evidence Says

Multiple RCTs support gratitude journaling. Effects are modest but consistent: increased optimism, greater life satisfaction, and in workplace contexts, improved engagement and reduced absenteeism. The 21-day recommendation comes from research on habit formation and the time needed for the scanning behavior to become more automatic.
